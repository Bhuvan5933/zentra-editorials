NGT's Green Light for Great Nicobar Project: A Critical Look

Proponents of the controversial Great Nicobar Project will be enthused by an order from the Kolkata bench of the National Green Tribunal (NGT) ruling that all environmental safeguards are in place, that the potential impact of this gargantuan project on resident native populations of the region is duly accounted for, and the project’s “strategic utility” is reason enough for the government to not be fully transparent with what it shares in the public domain. But the dominant narrative around the project mirrors the classic development versus environment conflict of a pristine Pandora being ravaged for the greed of far-away mainlanders. The Great Nicobar Island Project (GNIP) envisages a trans-shipment port, an international airport, township development, and a 450 Megavolt-Amperes (MVA) gas and solar-based power plant. In the early 20th century, the British Phosphate Commissioners (a joint venture of the U.K., Australia, and New Zealand) began large-scale phosphate mining for fertilizer on Nauru and Banaba in the Pacific Ocean. By 1945, the island had been so physically devastated by strip mining that it was deemed uninhabitable. The native Banabans were forcibly relocated to Rabi Island in Fiji, over 2,000 kilometres away. Today, Banaba is a desolate landscape of jagged limestone “pinnacles” and the displaced population is fighting for the rehabilitation of its homeland. These serve as historical precedents for why economic logic alone cannot dictate actions in remote territories. Though accorded an environment and preliminary forest clearance by the Union Environment Ministry, concerns about the potential loss of biodiversity, tree-felling, and impact on resident tribes prompted the NGT to order a review of the environmental aspects of the project. Independent scientists and environmentalist groups have said that the felling of tracts of pristine tropical forest — nearly nine lakh trees across 130 sq.km — for this project would significantly harm the biodiversity of the region and constitute an irreparable loss. This is not counting the disturbance to leatherback turtle nesting grounds and the assault on the corals. There was also the contested matter of whether the community rights of the local Shompen and the Nicobarese tribes were fully settled under the requirements of the Forest Rights Act. Recently, members of the Tribal Council said they were being coerced to sign “surrender certificates” that implied their consent to large parts of their land being diverted for the project. The NGT order essentially rubber-stamps the government’s appraisal process without independently examining the concerns raised. It only imposes faith that the government will be a conscientious executor of the project. Whether the Great Nicobar Project is a ‘net good’ can only be judged by future generations, but the lack of a process that offers a fair appraisal of independent concerns bodes ill for the present.

A. ERROR SPOTTING - TYPE 1

Q1. In each of the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one or more may contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence(s) that contains the grammatical or contextual error.

A) The NGT order essentially rubber-stamps the government’s appraisal process,

B) without independently examining the concerns raised.

C) It only imposes faith that the government will be a conscientious executor of the project.

D) Whether the Great Nicobar Project is a ‘net good’ can only be judged by future generations,

E) but the lack of a process that offers a fair appraisal of independent concerns bode ill for the present.

ANSWER: E

EXPLANATION:

The error is in part (E). The subject of the clause is 'the lack of a process', which is singular. Therefore, the verb should be 'bodes' (singular) instead of 'bode' (plural). The correct phrase is 'bodes ill for'.

Correct Sentence: but the lack of a process that offers a fair appraisal of independent concerns bodes ill for the present.

Q2. In each of the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one or more may contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence(s) that contains the grammatical or contextual error.

A) Proponents of the controversial Great Nicobar Project

B) will be enthused by an order from the Kolkata bench

C) of the National Green Tribunal (NGT) ruling that all environmental safeguards are in place,

D) that the potential impact of this gargantuan project on resident native populations of the region is duly accounted for,

E) and the project’s “strategic utility” is reason enough for the government to not being fully transparent.

ANSWER: E

EXPLANATION:

The error is in part (E). The phrase 'is reason enough for the government to...' requires an infinitive verb phrase, not a gerund. It should be 'to be fully transparent' instead of 'to not being fully transparent'. The correct structure is 'to + not + be + adjective/adverb'.

Correct Sentence: and the project’s “strategic utility” is reason enough for the government to not be fully transparent.

Correct Sentence: This is not counting the disturbance to leatherback turtle nesting grounds and the assault on the corals.

C. SENTENCE CORRECTION

Q11. In the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one may or may not contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence that is grammatically and contextually correct.

A) The committee's decision, which were met with disapproval, reflected a lack of understanding.

B) The committee's decision, which was met with disapproval, reflected a lack of understanding.

C) The committee's decision, which were met with disapprovals, reflected a lack of understanding.

D) The committee's decision, that was met with disapproval, reflected a lack of understanding.

E) The committee's decision, met with disapproval, reflecting a lack of understanding.

ANSWER: B

EXPLANATION:

Option (B) is the most grammatically correct. The relative pronoun 'which' refers to 'the committee's decision', a singular noun, so the verb 'was' (singular) is correct. The past participle 'reflected' correctly conveys the past action. Options (A) and (C) use the plural 'were' incorrectly. Option (D) uses 'that' with a comma, which is generally incorrect for non-restrictive clauses (commas usually pair with 'which'). Option (E) has a participial phrase 'reflecting' that doesn't clearly modify the 'decision' in the same way, making the sentence structure less clear and potentially incorrect.

Q12. In the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one may or may not contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence that is grammatically and contextually correct.

A) Environmentalists are fighting for the protection of pristine forests, but their efforts are often rubber-stamped by political will.

B) Environmentalists fights for the protection of pristine forests, but their efforts are often rubber-stamped by political will.

C) Environmentalists are fighting for the protection of pristine forests, but their efforts is often rubber-stamped by political will.

D) Environmentalists are fighting for the protection of pristine forests, however their efforts are often rubber-stamped by political will.

E) Environmentalists are fighting for the protection of pristine forests, but its efforts are often rubber-stamped by political will.

ANSWER: A

EXPLANATION:

Option (A) is grammatically correct. 'Environmentalists' is plural, so 'are fighting' is correct. 'Their efforts' is plural, so 'are often rubber-stamped' is also correct. 'But' is an appropriate conjunction to show contrast. Option (B) has subject-verb agreement error ('fights'). Option (C) has subject-verb agreement error ('efforts is'). Option (D) uses 'however' incorrectly without proper punctuation; 'however' usually needs a semicolon before it or should start a new sentence. Option (E) uses the singular possessive 'its' instead of the plural 'their'.

Q13. In the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one may or may not contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence that is grammatically and contextually correct.

A) The potential of technological advancements for sustainable development envisages a brighter future.

B) The potential of technological advancements for sustainable development envisage a brighter future.

C) The potential of technological advancements for sustainable development envisaging a brighter future.

D) The potential of technological advancements for sustainable development is envisaging a brighter future.

E) The potential of technological advancements for sustainable development, it envisages a brighter future.

ANSWER: A

EXPLANATION:

Option (A) is correct. The subject of the sentence is 'The potential' (singular), so the verb 'envisages' (singular) is correct. The phrase 'of technological advancements for sustainable development' modifies 'potential'. Option (B) uses the plural verb 'envisage' with a singular subject. Option (C) uses a participle 'envisaging' without a helping verb, making it an incomplete sentence. Option (D) uses the progressive tense 'is envisaging' which suggests an ongoing action, but 'potential' itself doesn't actively 'envisage' in this way; 'envisages' as a simple present conveys the inherent capacity. Option (E) creates a comma splice and an awkward structure with the redundant 'it'.

Q14. In the following questions, five sentences are given, out of which one may or may not contain a grammatical or contextual error. You are required to identify the sentence that is grammatically and contextually correct.

A) Concerns about the loss of biodiversity was prompted the NGT to order a review.

B) Concerns about the loss of biodiversity prompted the NGT to order a review.

C) Concerns about the loss of biodiversity prompting the NGT to order a review.

D) Concerns about the loss of biodiversity were prompted the NGT to order a review.

E) Concerns about the loss of biodiversity has prompted the NGT to order a review.

ANSWER: B

EXPLANATION:

Option (B) is the most correct. The subject 'Concerns' is plural, so the past tense verb 'prompted' (which doesn't change form for singular/plural in simple past) is correctly used. 'To order a review' is an infinitive phrase. Option (A) incorrectly uses 'was prompted' (passive voice, incorrect here). Option (C) is a sentence fragment as 'prompting' is a participle without a main verb. Option (D) uses 'were prompted', which suggests the concerns themselves were prompted (passive voice), rather than the concerns *doing* the prompting. Option (E) uses 'has prompted', which would be singular, but 'concerns' is plural, so it would need 'have prompted' for present perfect, but simple past 'prompted' is more appropriate for a specific past action.

Q49. In the question below, the word CONSTITUTE is given, followed by three sentences that use the word. Identify the sentence(s) in which the word has been used correctly.

(I) These three ingredients constitute the basic recipe for the dish.

A) Only I B) Only II C) I and III D) II and III E) All are correct

ANSWER: I, II and III

EXPLANATION:

All three sentences use the word 'constitute' correctly. In (I), 'constitute' means to form or make up. In (II), it means to amount to or represent. In (III), it means to establish or set up, often formally.

Q50. In the question below, the word IMPLIES is given, followed by three sentences that use the word. Identify the sentence(s) in which the word has been used correctly.

ANSWER: I, II and III

EXPLANATION:

All three sentences use 'implies' correctly. 'Implies' means to suggest or hint at something without directly stating it. In all cases, a conclusion is drawn or a suggestion is made based on available information or actions.

Q51. In the question below, the word DOMINANT is given, followed by three sentences that use the word. Identify the sentence(s) in which the word has been used correctly.

(III) The artist used a dominant shade of blue throughout his latest collection.

ANSWER: I, II and III

EXPLANATION:

All three sentences use 'dominant' correctly. In (I), it refers to being the most powerful or influential. In (II), it means being the most important or successful. In (III), it refers to a prominent or most noticeable feature (color in this case).

Q52. In the question below, the word CONSCIENTIOUS is given, followed by three sentences that use the word. Identify the sentence(s) in which the word has been used correctly.

(I) A conscientious student always strives for excellence in their studies.

(II) The doctor was conscientious in his duties, always putting his patients first.

(III) He showed a conscientious disregard for safety regulations, leading to an accident.

A) Only I B) Only II C) I and III D) II and III E) All are correct

ANSWER: I and II

EXPLANATION:

Sentences (I) and (II) use 'conscientious' correctly to mean wishing to do what is right and performing duties thoroughly. Sentence (III) uses 'conscientious' incorrectly. 'Conscientious disregard' is an oxymoron; 'conscientious' implies carefulness and adherence to duty, while 'disregard' means lack of care. A correct usage would be 'blatant disregard' or 'reckless disregard'.
